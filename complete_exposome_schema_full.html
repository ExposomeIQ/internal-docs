Complete Exposome Schema — all four domains: Chemical (xenobiotics A–N and endobiotics A–I), Biological (pathogens, microbiome, allergens, biotoxins), Psychosocial (A–G), and Physical (A–F) with full subclasses and mechanisms

EXPOSOME
All non-genetic influences on human health — from conception onward

Chemical Exposome
Xenobiotics — classified by biological effect — 14 categories
AGenotoxic
Mutagens
Aflatoxin B1, benzene, ethylene oxide, PAHs (benzo[a]pyrene)
DNA adducts, base-pair substitutions via CYP450 bioactivation
Clastogens
IR byproducts, organochlorines, benzene metabolites
Chromosome strand breakage
Aneugens
Colchicine-like compounds, some pesticides
Spindle disruption → wrong chromosome count in daughter cells

Direct-acting
Nitrogen mustards, bis-chloromethyl ether
No metabolic activation required
Procarcinogens
Benzo[a]pyrene, aflatoxin B1, nitrosamines

Tumor promoters non-genotoxic
TCDD/dioxins, phorbol esters, chlorinated HCs, chronic estrogen excess

Asbestos, benzene, formaldehyde, vinyl chloride, arsenic, cadmium, PCBs, TCDD, aflatoxins, trichloroethylene

Morphological teratogens
Thalidomide, retinoids (high dose), valproic acid, mercury, dioxins

Functional teratogens

Ion channel disruptors

Excitotoxic / glutamate
Domoic acid, mycotoxins, certain solvents
Axonal / myelinopathic

Direct injury / radical-mediated

Steatosis / NAFLD
PFAS, amiodarone (phospholipidosis), alcohol (ethanol)

Tubular toxins
Cadmium (metallothionein accumulation → Fanconi syndrome / itai-itai), mercury, cisplatin
Nephropathy-inducing

Mycotoxin nephrotoxins

Other
Melamine (precipitates in tubules — 2008 milk scandal), PFAS, contrast media

Immunosuppressants

Immunostimulants / sensitizers

Hypertensive

Atherogenic / thrombogenic
PFAS (dyslipidemia, hypertension, atherosclerosis); PAHs / PM2.5 (oxidative stress, platelet activation → acute MI)
Other
Dioxins, some organochlorines

Fibrogenic / carcinogenic
Asbestos (mesothelioma, asbestosis — mechanical + inflammatory), silica (silicosis)

Bronchiolitis obliterans
Diacetyl (popcorn lung — butter flavoring); isocyanates (occupational asthma)
Inflammatory

Bone marrow toxins

Arsenic → hemolysis

CNS depressants

Stimulants
Cocaine (dopamine reuptake blockade), amphetamines (DA/NE release)
Psychedelics
LSD, psilocybin (serotonin receptors), THC (CB1 receptor)
Environmental behavioral toxins
Organophosphates (chronic anxiety/depression at sub-acute levels), PCBs (dopaminergic disruption), lead/mercury (cognitive impairment without acute symptoms)
MObesogenic / Metabolic
PPARγ activators
Tributyltin (TBT) — master regulator of fat cell differentiation; coined "obesogens" (Grün & Blumberg, 2006)
Pancreatic / insulin disruptors

Thyroid-metabolic disruptors
Phthalates → thyroid disruption → altered basal metabolic rate
Lipid / insulin resistance
PFAS (altered lipid profiles, insulin resistance), atrazine (metabolic syndrome, T2D)
T2D epidemiological links
PCBs, organochlorine pesticides — strong epidemiological correlation with type 2 diabetes
NEpigenetic
AhR-mediated gene silencing
TCDD/dioxins → AhR → HDAC complexes → immune/metabolic gene loci altered
Imprinted gene methylation

Oncogenic epigenome reprogramming
Arsenic → oncogene hypomethylation + tumor suppressor hypermethylation → cancer-permissive state
Transposon dysregulation
PM2.5 → hypomethylation of LINE-1 repetitive elements
Transgenerational inheritance ≥4 gen
Vinclozolin (fungicide) → epigenetic program transmitted via male germline for ≥4 generations (Skinner et al., Nature, 2005)
Xenobiotic gone — inherited epigenetic program remains
Endobiotics — body's own chemicals at abnormal levels; xenobiotic-induced dysregulation — 9 categories
ASteroid Hormones
Produced from cholesterol; same CYP450 enzymes that xenobiotics exploit
Glucocorticoids (cortisol, cortisone)
Disrupted by: organotins, TCDD, PFAS
Mineralocorticoids (aldosterone)
Disrupted by xenobiotics activating/blocking mineralocorticoid receptor
Estrogens (E2, E1, E3)
Xenoestrogens mimic E2 at ERα/ERβ; atrazine ↑aromatase → endogenous estrogen excess; EDCs alter E2/E1/E3 ratio, suppress SHBG → ↑free estrogen fraction
Androgens (testosterone, DHT, DHEA)
Anti-androgenic phthalates suppress Leydig cell StAR + CYP17A1 → ↓fetal testosterone; vinclozolin metabolites block androgen receptor directly
Progestogens (progesterone)
Disrupted secondarily via estrogen imbalance; some synthetic progestin-like xenobiotics
BPeptide / Protein Hormones
Thyroid hormones (T4, T3)
Perchlorate competes with iodide for NIS → ↓iodine uptake → ↓T4/T3; PBDEs structurally mimic T4, compete for transthyretin; triclosan inhibits deiodinase → impaired T4→T3 conversion
Insulin / glucagon
BPA → β-cell dysfunction + inappropriate secretion; PFAS → insulin resistance; organochlorine pesticides alter GLUT4 and adipokine secretion
Leptin / adiponectin / ghrelin
BPA, phthalates, PFAS alter adipokine secretion; TBT creates excess adipose → dysregulated adipokine output
Growth hormone / IGF-1
Disrupted by TCDD, heavy metals
LH / FSH (pituitary gonadotropins)
EDCs alter hypothalamic GnRH pulsatility; disrupt estrogen/androgen feedback → suppress LH/FSH surges needed for ovulation
CNeurotransmitters
Acetylcholine (ACh)
OPs/carbamates → AChE inhibition → acute SLUDGE syndrome; chronic → depression, cognitive decline
Dopamine
Manganese → basal ganglia depletion → Parkinsonism; lead/mercury → ↓synthesis + receptor density; PCBs → prefrontal dopamine → ↓executive function in children
Serotonin (5-HT)
OPs → ↓receptor expression; PFAS → altered serotonin metabolism; pesticides disrupt gut microbiome → tryptophan pathway disruption
GABA
Organochlorines (endosulfan, lindane) → GABA antagonism → neuronal hyperexcitability → seizures; alcohol chronic → GABA receptor downregulation → withdrawal seizures on cessation
Glutamate / Norepinephrine
Xenobiotic ROS → glutamate-glutamine cycling disruption → excitotoxic neuronal death; heavy metals, OPs, solvents alter NE
DEicosanoids (Lipid Mediators)
From arachidonic acid via COX and LOX pathways; short-lived local signaling
Prostaglandins (PGE2, PGI2)
PAHs, PM, dioxins, heavy metals → NF-κB → COX-2 upregulation → ↑PGE2
Chronic ↑PGE2 = major mechanism of xenobiotic-driven inflammation + tumor promotion
Thromboxanes (TXA2)
Xenobiotic ROS → prostacyclin/TXA2 imbalance → prothrombotic state
Air pollution PM is well-documented driver of this shift → ↑acute CV events
Leukotrienes (LTB4, cysteinyl LTs)
Xenobiotic ROS → ↑leukotriene synthesis; isocyanates → Th2 shift → asthma leukotriene overproduction
EReactive Oxygen / Nitrogen Species
Most important class produced directly by xenobiotic exposure; dysregulated from normal signaling into tissue-damaging overproduction
Key species
O2•− (superoxide), H2O2, •OH (hydroxyl), NO, ONOO− (peroxynitrite)
Xenobiotic induction mechanisms
Redox cycling (paraquat, doxorubicin); mitochondrial uncoupling (pentachlorophenol); CYP450 uncoupling; NF-κB → macrophage/neutrophil NADPH oxidase burst
Downstream consequences
Lipid peroxidation → MDA, 4-HNE, isoprostanes (secondary genotoxins + signaling mediators); protein carbonylation; 8-oxodG; oxidized LDL → atherosclerosis
FCytokines & Immune Mediators
Pro-inflammatory triad
TNF-α, IL-1β, IL-6
Upregulated by dioxins, PAHs, heavy metals, PM, phthalates, BPA via NF-κB → "inflammaging" → insulin resistance, atherosclerosis, cancer progression
Anti-inflammatory (suppressed)
IL-10, TGF-β — immunotoxic xenobiotics suppress these → removes brake on inflammation
Interferons
Altered by dioxins (AhR pathway), PFAS
Net effect
Chronic low-grade systemic cytokine dysregulation = unifying mechanism linking toxic chemical exposure to entire chronic disease spectrum
GBile Acids
Cholesterol metabolites; FXR/TGR5 nuclear receptor signaling; enterohepatic circulation; gut–brain axis regulators
Xenobiotic displacement
PFAS, PCBs displace bile acids from transporter proteins; inhibit bile acid conjugation enzymes
Disrupts FXR-mediated negative feedback → bile acid accumulation
Cytotoxic secondary bile acid accumulation
Deoxycholic acid (DCA), lithocholic acid (LCA) in colon → act as tumor promoters
Microbiome link
Gut dysbiosis from xenobiotics → altered primary→secondary bile acid conversion → colorectal cancer risk via combined dysbiosis + cytotoxic bile acid exposure
HEndogenous DNA Adducts
Produced from normal metabolism; dramatically amplified by xenobiotic-induced ROS; sit at the xenobiotic/endobiotic boundary
MDA-DNA adducts (M1G)
Malondialdehyde from lipid peroxidation → deoxyguanosine adducts → mutagenic
4-HNE-DNA adducts
4-Hydroxynonenal from LPO; also recognized as a bona fide signaling molecule
8-oxodG
•OH attacks guanine → G→T transversions; most widely used oxidative DNA damage biomarker
Etheno-DNA adducts
1,N6-ethenoadenine, 3,N4-ethenocytosine — from LPO products; dramatically elevated by vinyl chloride and xenobiotic-induced oxidative stress generally
IEpigenetic Marks
Endogenous modifications written/erased by enzymes (DNMTs, HDACs, HATs) that xenobiotics directly alter; can be transgenerationally inherited
DNA methylation (5-mC)
Arsenic: oncogene hypomethylation + TSG hypermethylation → cancer-permissive epigenome
BPA: altered methylation at imprinted genes (Igf2, H19) in fetal development → permanent metabolic reprogramming
Histone modification
TCDD → AhR activation → HDAC complex recruitment → gene cluster silencing
Non-coding RNA
Multiple xenobiotics alter miRNA expression profiles → post-transcriptional gene dysregulation
Transgenerational inheritance ≥4 gen
Vinclozolin (fungicide) → male germline epigenetic program inherited ≥4 generations (Skinner et al., Nature, 2005)
Bridges to psychosocial domain: ACEs → NR3C1 methylation → ↓GR → sustained HPA activation
Biological Exposome — Pathogens, Microbiome, Allergens, Biotoxins
A. Pathogens — 4 classes
A1Viruses
Cytopathic
Influenza (airway epithelium lysis), HIV (CD4+ T-cell depletion → AIDS), poliovirus (motor neuron death), HBV/HCV (hepatocyte lysis)
DAMPs release → NF-κB → cytokine storm (TNF-α, IL-6, IL-1β, interferons)
Oncogenic
HPV 16/18 (cervical/oropharyngeal — E6/E7 inactivate p53 + Rb); EBV (Burkitt's lymphoma, NPC); HBV/HCV (HCC via chronic inflammation); HTLV-1 (T-cell leukemia); KSHV/HHV-8 (Kaposi's sarcoma)
Immunosuppressive
HIV (destroys CD4+ helper T cells); measles (immunological amnesia — erases B-cell memory for months post-infection)
Neurotropic
Rabies (retrograde axonal transport → fatal encephalitis); HSV-1 (latent in trigeminal ganglia; associated with Alzheimer's); West Nile (meningitis/encephalitis); SARS-CoV-2 (microglial activation, neuroinflammation, ACE2 neuroinvasion, vascular injury → Long COVID cognitive effects)
Teratogenic
Rubella (cardiac defects, cataracts, deafness, intellectual disability); Zika (microcephaly — infects neural progenitor cells); CMV (most common congenital viral infection → sensorineural hearing loss, cognitive impairment)
Respiratory
SARS-CoV-2, influenza, RSV → pulmonary epithelial damage, endothelial injury, microthrombi, systemic cytokine elevation
A2Bacteria
Toxin-producing — exotoxins
C. botulinum (SNARE cleavage → flaccid paralysis); C. tetani (blocks glycine/GABA → spastic paralysis); V. cholerae (locks Gs protein → massive cAMP → dehydrating diarrhea); S. aureus (superantigens → 20% T-cell activation → toxic shock); C. diphtheriae (ADP-ribosylates EF-2 → ↓protein synthesis → myocarditis)
Endotoxins (LPS)
Gram-negative outer membrane; TLR4 → NF-κB; acute: septic shock; chronic low-level ("metabolic endotoxemia") → obesity, insulin resistance, CVD
Invasive
M. tuberculosis (granuloma, lung destruction, dissemination to any organ); S. pneumoniae (pneumonia, meningitis); T. pallidum (syphilis — tertiary: CV + neurological); L. monocytogenes (crosses BBB and placenta)
Carcinogenic
H. pylori (IARC Group 1 — gastric adenocarcinoma + MALT lymphoma via CagA toxin, chronic inflammation, ↑proliferation/↓apoptosis)
Acute-phase response: ↑CRP, fibrinogen, SAA; fever via PGE2/COX-2; cortisol via HPA axis (sickness behavior)
A3Fungi
Mycotoxin-producing molds
Aspergillus → aflatoxins (IARC 1 — HCC); Fusarium → trichothecenes (protein synthesis inhibition, immunosuppression, GI damage); Alternaria → alternariol (genotoxic); Stachybotrys ("black mold") → satratoxins (respiratory inflammation)
Invasive fungal infections
Candida albicans (systemic candidiasis — biofilm formation, epithelial barrier disruption); A. fumigatus (angioinvasive → necrosis via hyphal vascular invasion); Cryptococcus neoformans (cryptococcal meningitis — polysaccharide capsule inhibits phagocytosis)
Primarily in immunocompromised individuals
Allergenic fungi
Cladosporium, Alternaria, Aspergillus, Penicillium → IgE sensitization → Th2 polarization → allergic rhinitis, asthma
A4Parasites
Protozoa
Plasmodium (malaria — RBC destruction, cerebral malaria via brain microvasculature sequestration; hemozoin → TLR9-mediated inflammation); Toxoplasma gondii (latent brain infection — alters dopaminergic signaling, associated with schizophrenia risk); Trypanosoma (sleeping sickness, Chagas disease — cardiomyopathy, neurodegeneration); Leishmania (granulomatous skin/visceral disease)
Helminths
Drive Th2 polarization (IL-4, IL-5, IL-13, IgE) — protective against autoimmunity (hygiene hypothesis)
Ascaris (intestinal); Schistosoma (IARC Group 1 — hepatic fibrosis; S. haematobium → bladder cancer); Toxocara (ocular/visceral larval migrans); Echinococcus (hydatid cysts — liver/lung)
B–D. Microbiome, Allergens & Biotoxins
BMicrobiome — a distinct sub-exposome
~38 trillion microorganisms; simultaneously an endobiotic factory, barrier, immune educator, and xenobiotic metabolizer
Metabolic — SCFAs
Dietary fiber fermentation → butyrate (colonocyte fuel, gut barrier integrity, HDAC inhibition = epigenetic modulator), propionate, acetate
Dysbiosis → ↓SCFAs → IBD, colorectal cancer, obesity, metabolic syndrome
Immune education
Calibrates Th1/Th2/Th17/Treg balance in early life critical window
Dysbiosis → autoimmune disease (IBD, MS, T1D), allergic disease, ↓vaccine immunogenicity
Gut–brain axis
~90% of body serotonin gut-derived; GABA + dopamine precursors produced by bacteria; vagal signaling via SCFAs
Dysbiosis → depression, anxiety, ASD; Parkinson's (alpha-synuclein aggregation begins in enteric NS); Alzheimer's
Xenobiotic metabolism
Bacteria activate, detoxify, or reactivate liver-conjugated xenobiotics (bidirectional relationship)
Microbiome = both target of xenobiotic damage AND determinant of xenobiotic toxicity
Dysbiosis pathway
Triggers: antibiotics, ultra-processed diet, xenobiotic exposure, psychosocial stress
→ ↑Intestinal permeability → LPS translocation (metabolic endotoxemia) → chronic low-grade systemic inflammation → CVD, neurodegeneration, metabolic disorders
CAllergens & Immune-Provoking Agents
Aeroallergens
Pollen (grass, tree, weed); house dust mite (Der p 1 — serine protease disrupts epithelial tight junctions); mold spores; animal dander
IgE sensitization → mast cell/basophil degranulation → histamine, PGs, LTs → rhinitis, asthma, anaphylaxis
Food allergens
Big 9: peanut (Ara h proteins), shellfish (tropomyosin), milk, egg, wheat, tree nuts, soy, fish, sesame
Type I (immediate IgE-mediated) to Type IV (T-cell mediated — celiac disease via gliadin)
Contact allergens
Nickel, chromate, urushiol (poison ivy)
Type IV delayed-type hypersensitivity — sensitized T cells attack hapten-protein complexes in skin
Insect venoms
Bee/wasp/ant: phospholipase A2, melittin, hyaluronidase
Direct membrane toxicity + IgE-mediated anaphylaxis in sensitized individuals
DBiotoxins — biological–chemical exposome boundary
Marine biotoxins
Saxitoxin (paralytic shellfish — blocks voltage-gated Na+ channels → paralysis); domoic acid (amnesic shellfish — glutamate receptor agonist → hippocampal excitotoxicity → memory loss); brevetoxins (neurotoxic shellfish); ciguatoxin (fish — Na+ channel opener → cold allodynia, chronic neurological)
Produced by dinoflagellates/cyanobacteria; human exposure via seafood
Cyanobacterial toxins
Microcystin (hepatotoxic — inhibits PP1/PP2A; potential IARC carcinogen); cylindrospermopsin (hepatotoxic, genotoxic); anatoxin-a (nicotinic agonist → rapid NM paralysis)
Mycotoxins
Aflatoxins (IARC 1 — HCC); ochratoxin A (nephrotoxic + renal carcinogen); trichothecenes (protein synthesis inhibition); zearalenone (estrogenic); fumonisins; patulin
Plant biotoxins
Ricin (Ricinus communis — ribosomal protein synthesis inhibition, extreme toxicity); abrin; solanine (Solanum); coniine (hemlock — nicotinic receptor antagonist → paralysis); taxine (yew — cardiac toxicity)
Psychosocial Exposome — Social environment as biological environment
Key transduction pathway: HPA axis (CRH → ACTH → cortisol) + SAM system (sympathetic → epinephrine/NE). Concept: allostatic load (McEwen & Stellar, 1993) — cumulative physiological wear from chronic stress response activation.
AAcute Stress Exposures
Short-duration, high-intensity; biological response is adaptive and largely reversible
Psychological threat (danger, fear, performance pressure)
Amygdala → hypothalamus → CRH → pituitary ACTH → adrenal cortisol; simultaneous sympathetic → adrenal medulla → epinephrine/NE
Effects: ↑HR, ↑BP, glucose mobilization, immune activation (initial), coagulation priming, memory consolidation enhancement
Acute trauma / injury
Cortisol spike + massive catecholamine release; inflammatory cascade; substance P + CGRP release (pain signaling)
BChronic Stress Exposures
Sustained activation without recovery — converges on same molecular pathways as toxic chemical exposures
Occupational stress / overload
Chronic cortisol → hippocampal neurogenesis suppression → gray matter loss (MRI-documented); cortisol-induced insulin resistance; CVD via catecholamine-driven arterial inflammation
Poverty / material deprivation
Constant threat-system activation → cortisol dysregulation (elevated → blunted in severe chronic poverty, analogous to receptor downregulation from chronic agonist); ↑CRP, IL-6, TNF-α; shortened telomere length; ↑epigenetic aging rate
Caregiver stress
Documented telomere shortening in primary caregivers — among first demonstrations that psychosocial burden produces measurable biological aging
Discrimination & racism
↑Allostatic load; hypertension → "weathering" hypothesis (accelerated biological aging); ↑cortisol reactivity; ↑inflammatory markers
Now classified as chronic psychosocial stressor with measurable biological embedding
CEarly Life Adversity — ACEs
Most consequential category — early life = critical developmental plasticity window; exposures permanently biologically embedded; reshape stress response architecture
ACE types
Physical/sexual/emotional abuse; neglect; household violence; parental mental illness, substance abuse, or incarceration; parental separation or death
HPA axis reprogramming
Calibrates cortisol reactivity for life → hyperreactivity (hypervigilance) OR hypoactivity in severe chronic cases (system shutdown). Both patterns confer health risk.
Immune system reprogramming
Elevated adult CRP, IL-6, inflammatory markers persist after controlling for adult health behaviors → permanent immune recalibration, not explained by lifestyle
Epigenetic changes
↑NR3C1 methylation → ↓glucocorticoid receptor expression → ↓cortisol feedback → sustained HPA activation; FKBP5 methylation (PTSD vulnerability gene); BDNF methylation (↓neuroplasticity); telomere shortening
Brain structural changes
↓Hippocampal volume; ↓prefrontal cortex gray matter; ↑amygdala size → threat-detection-biased neurobiological architecture, impaired emotional regulation
DSocial Isolation & Loneliness
Social isolation/loneliness → +26–29% mortality risk — comparable to smoking 15 cigarettes/day (Holt-Lunstad meta-analyses)
Inflammatory dysregulation — CTRA
Conserved Transcriptional Response to Adversity: ↑IL-1β, IL-6, IL-8, TNF-α; ↓antiviral interferon responses
Evolutionary logic: anticipate bacterial wounds from social conflict. Maladaptive in modern chronic isolation.
HPA / cortisol
↑Basal cortisol, blunted cortisol awakening response → progressive HPA dysregulation
Cardiovascular effects
↑BP, accelerated atherosclerosis, ↑fibrinogen + CRP, higher stroke + CAD risk
Immune suppression
↓NK cell activity; ↓vaccine antibody response; ↓T-cell proliferation → ↑susceptibility to viral infections
Telomere / sleep
Telomere shortening + ↓telomerase; independently predicts poor sleep quality → cascading inflammatory/metabolic dysregulation
EGrief, Bereavement & Acute Loss
Takotsubo (stress) cardiomyopathy
Acute psychological shock → massive catecholamine surge → Ca2+ overload → mitochondrial dysfunction → transient severe LV dysfunction mimicking MI
Epinephrine/NE at extreme concentrations = directly cardiomyocyte-toxic
Bereavement systemic effects
↓NK + T-cell activity; ↑cortisol; impaired wound healing; disrupted sleep-wake cycles
Widowhood mortality effect
Dramatically elevated mortality risk in weeks following spousal death; predominantly cardiovascular
FPTSD & Chronic Anxiety Disorders
PTSD — biological hallmarks
↓Hippocampal volume; blunted basal cortisol BUT hyperreactive cortisol to triggers; ↑sympathetic tone/NE; altered glucocorticoid receptor sensitivity; ↑inflammatory markers
Failure of fear extinction → amygdala in sustained threat-detection mode → chronically unresolved stress response
Chronic anxiety disorders
Sustained low-level HPA + sympathetic activation → chronically elevated cortisol; suppressed immune function; cardiovascular risk elevation
GSocial Determinants as Biological Exposures — "place as biology"
Educational attainment
↑Education → ↓allostatic load via multiple pathways: health literacy, income, stress coping capacity, social network quality
Neighborhood quality
Neighborhood-level poverty, violence exposure, and green space access all have measurable biological correlates in resident cortisol levels and inflammatory markers
Food insecurity / housing instability / healthcare access
Chronic threat-state activation; interacts with and amplifies all other psychosocial stressor pathways; structural poverty as chronic biological stressor
Physical Exposome — Non-chemical, non-biological environmental forces
Act through energy transfer, not molecular binding. Downstream effects converge on same endobiotic responses: ROS generation, DNA damage, inflammation, heat shock protein induction.
AIonizing Radiation (IR)
Types
Alpha (high damage, low penetrance — dangerous from ingested/inhaled sources: radon, plutonium); Beta; Gamma/X-rays (high penetrance); Neutrons (nuclear reactions)
Sources
Natural background ~50% avg US exposure (cosmic + radon in soil); medical imaging (CT chest = ~7 mSv ≈ 2 yrs background); nuclear medicine; occupational (nuclear, radiology); Chernobyl/Fukushima fallout
Biological mechanisms
Direct: ionizes DNA → double-strand breaks (most dangerous lesion); Indirect: ionizes H2O → •OH → attacks DNA (~60–70% of damage)
Downstream endobiotic effects
DSBs → ATM kinase → p53 → cell cycle arrest or apoptosis; lipid peroxidation → 4-HNE/MDA; NF-κB → inflammation; bystander effect: irradiated cells release ROS + cytokines → damage neighboring unirradiated cells
Health effects
High acute: Acute Radiation Syndrome (BM failure, GI, CNS); chronic low: ↑cancer risk (leukemia, solid tumors); radon = 2nd leading cause of lung cancer; IARC Group 1
Protective biological response
HSPs, antioxidant enzymes (catalase, SOD, GPx), DNA repair pathways (NHEJ, HR)
BNon-Ionizing Radiation — 5 subtypes
B1: UV Radiation
UVB: thymine absorption → cyclobutane pyrimidine dimers (CPDs) + 6-4 photoproducts → C→T / CC→TT mutations ("UV signature") → melanoma, SCC, BCC; repaired by NER
UVA: deeper penetration → ROS → 8-oxodG, lipid peroxidation; depletes Langerhans cells → skin immunosuppression
Benefit: UVB → vitamin D3 synthesis from 7-dehydrocholesterol (endobiotic from physical stimulus)
B2: Infrared (IR)
Thermal tissue effects; protein denaturation at high exposure; chronic near-IR (glassblowers, welders) → ↑cataract risk; photobiomodulation (red light) → stimulates cytochrome c oxidase → ↑ATP, ↓ROS
B3: Visible light / Blue light / Light at Night (LAN)
Blue light (400–490nm): activates ipRGCs (melanopsin) → ↓melatonin → circadian disruption → sleep suppression, ↑evening cortisol, impaired glucose metabolism, immune dysregulation
LAN/light pollution: ↑breast cancer risk (melatonin suppression — antioxidant + oncostatic); obesity, CVD, neurodegeneration; LAN linked to accelerated MCI→dementia conversion (Filippini 2024)
B4: RF / Microwave
Mobile phones, WiFi, cell towers, radar; IARC 2B (glioma — 2011, contested, under revision)
Non-ionizing → cannot break bonds; proposed: thermal effects (high exposure); non-thermal via voltage-gated Ca2+ channels; ↑mitochondrial ROS; circadian disruption via melatonin
B5: ELF-EMF (power lines, appliances)
IARC 2B (childhood leukemia — epidemiological association at ≥0.3–0.4 µT)
Mechanism not established; proposed: melatonin suppression, free radical pair reactions
CNoise / Acoustic Exposure
WHO: 2nd largest environmental health hazard in Europe after air pollution
Auditory effects
Cochlear hair cell damage via mechanical trauma + ROS → glutamate excitotoxicity at auditory synapse → sensorineural hearing loss
Occupational threshold typically >85 dB(A) averaged over 8hr
Cardiovascular (non-auditory)
Nighttime noise even below consciousness threshold → sympathetic activation → cortisol + catecholamine release → nocturnal BP elevation → ↑MI, stroke risk
Hyena Study (2017, Berlin-Tegel): nighttime aircraft noise → ↑urinary NE + adrenaline → ↑CV events
Cognitive effects
Children near airports/motorways: impaired learning, memory consolidation (disrupted slow-wave sleep), executive function deficits
Mediated by: sleep disruption, cortisol elevation, direct attentional interference during learning
DTemperature Extremes
Heat stress
HSF1 activation → heat shock proteins (HSP70, HSP90, HSP27 — molecular chaperones); sustained heat → vasodilation → ↓preload → cardiac output strain; electrolyte loss → hyponatremia, rhabdomyolysis
Heat stroke (>40°C core): endothelial damage, cytokine storm, coagulopathy, multi-organ failure
27-country analysis (Alahmad et al., Circulation 2023): extreme heat → substantial excess CV mortality; climate change ↑risk
Cold exposure
Sympathetic vasoconstriction → ↑peripheral vascular resistance → ↑BP; ↑blood viscosity + platelet aggregation → prothrombotic (higher MI/stroke in cold months)
Severe hypothermia (<35°C): ventricular fibrillation, coagulopathy, immune suppression
HSPs as exposome-induced endobiotics
Extracellular HSPs released from stressed cells recognized by TLRs → inflammatory response → links thermal stress to atherosclerosis pathogenesis
EHypoxia / Altitude / Pressure
HIF-1α pathway — master response
HIF-1α activated when cellular O2 drops → transcribes: EPO (↑RBC production), VEGF (angiogenesis), glycolytic enzymes (→ anaerobic metabolism), GLUT transporters
Also exploited by tumor cells to drive their own vascularization
Chronic mountain sickness (Monge's disease)
Chronic altitude → polycythemia (sustained EPO → ↑viscosity → thrombosis + pulmonary hypertension)
Sleep apnea (intermittent hypoxia)
Repeated HIF-1α activation cycles at sea level → chronic cardiovascular consequences identical to altitude mechanisms
Hyperbaric oxygen
High pressure: O2 toxicity via ROS; therapeutic applications: wound healing, CO poisoning reversal, decompression sickness, radiation injury repair
FPhysical Forces (Mechanical)
Vibration
Whole-body (heavy vehicle drivers) → low back pain, intervertebral disc degeneration (disrupts proteoglycan synthesis, accelerates disc cell apoptosis)
Hand-arm vibration (power tools) → vibration white finger / Raynaud's → vascular spasm + peripheral nerve damage
Mechanical trauma / injury
Cell damage → DAMP release (HMGB1, ATP, uric acid) → TLR + inflammasome activation → IL-1β, IL-18, TNF-α; coagulation cascade; TGF-β / fibroblast-mediated tissue remodeling
Repetitive concussive trauma
Contact sports, blast injury → CTE (chronic traumatic encephalopathy): tau protein accumulation, neuroinflammation, progressive brain atrophy
DAMP-mediated sterile inflammation; progressive neurodegeneration from repeated sub-threshold injury accumulation
